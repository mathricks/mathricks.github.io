Why Simple Rules Create Hard Puzzles

“The rules are simple.”

For a puzzle game, that sentence is rarely a promise that the game will be easy. More often, it is the warning sign of something much more interesting: a small system that can generate a very large number of consequences.

Sudoku asks you to place the digits 1 to 9 without repetition. Wordle gives you six attempts to identify a five-letter word. Tetris drops seven kinds of pieces into a rectangular well. Minesweeper tells you how many hidden mines surround a square. Chess begins with 32 pieces and a board of 64 squares.

None of these games needs a long manual. Yet they have produced decades of obsession, study, competition, software solvers, strategy guides and daily rituals. The important lesson is not simply that “simple things can be fun.” It is more precise than that:

A good puzzle does not get its depth from having many rules. It gets its depth from making a few rules interact in many ways.

Small rules, huge possibility spaces

Sudoku is a clean example. Its core rule can be explained in one sentence: each row, column and 3-by-3 box must contain the digits 1 through 9 exactly once. There is no arithmetic involved. The digits could be replaced by letters or symbols and the puzzle would still work.

But that simple constraint produces an enormous possibility space. Bertram Felgenhauer and Frazer Jarvis calculated that there are 6,670,903,752,021,072,936,960 possible completed Sudoku grids. If you remove grids that are essentially the same under relabeling, rotation and other symmetries, there are still 5,472,730,538 essentially different grids. (afjarvis.org.uk)

That is the first principle of good puzzle design: simplicity at the surface does not imply simplicity underneath. The player sees a 9-by-9 grid. The designer is working with a vast combinatorial system.

The same is true in chess. The rules of movement are simple enough for children to learn, but the number of possible games is famously astronomical. Claude Shannon used an estimate of around 10^120 possible chess games to show why brute-force search was not a practical way to “solve” chess. (Wikipedia)

The exact number is less important than the design lesson: chess is not deep because its rules are complicated. It is deep because every move changes the meaning of every future move.

Difficulty comes from interaction, not decoration

Many weak puzzle designs try to create difficulty by adding more rules, more exceptions, more special cases and more explanation. That can make a game harder to understand, but not necessarily more interesting.

The strongest puzzles often do the opposite. They keep the rule set small, then let the consequences become difficult.

Tetris is a perfect case. There are only seven tetrominoes. The player can move, rotate and drop them. Completed lines disappear. That is basically the game.

But every piece changes the future shape of the board. One small compromise creates a bump. A bump creates a gap. A gap forces a risky placement. A risky placement limits the next piece. The board becomes a physical memory of earlier decisions.

This is not just a poetic observation. Computer scientists have formally studied Tetris and shown that even offline versions of the game, where the sequence of pieces is known in advance, are computationally hard. Demaine, Hohenberger and Liben-Nowell proved that several optimization goals in offline Tetris, such as maximizing cleared rows or surviving as long as possible, are NP-complete. (arXiv)

That does not mean a normal human game of Tetris is “an NP-complete problem” in the casual sense. But it does show something important: under the friendly surface of falling blocks is a system capable of producing genuinely hard planning problems.

Constraints are the engine

A puzzle is not made interesting by giving the player infinite freedom. It is made interesting by giving the player meaningful constraints.

Wordle has five-letter words, six guesses and three kinds of feedback: correct letter in the correct position, correct letter in the wrong position and letter not present. That is a tiny design space compared with most modern games. But the constraints are doing serious work.

The five-letter limit makes each guess compact. The six-guess limit creates pressure. The colored feedback turns language into information. The daily puzzle format turns a solitary word game into a shared cultural object.

That last point mattered enormously. Wordle was not just playable, it was explainable. The New York Times acquired Wordle in January 2022 for a low seven-figure sum and later said the acquisition brought in “tens of millions” of new users. (TechCrunch)

The design lesson is clear: a puzzle that can be explained quickly has a much better chance of spreading. A puzzle that stays interesting after it has been explained has a chance of lasting.

This is why so many successful puzzle games have a one-sentence pitch:

  • Sudoku: fill the grid so every row, column and box contains 1 to 9.
  • Wordle: guess the five-letter word in six tries.
  • Minesweeper: use the numbers to avoid the mines.
  • 2048: slide matching tiles together to make bigger numbers.
  • Tetris: fit falling blocks together to clear lines.

None of these pitches contains the real depth of the game. That is exactly why they work. The rules get you in the door. The consequences keep you there.

We embrace this exact philosophy at Mathricks Studio. The core constraints of our own games can also be summarized in just one sentence:

  • Gridlock Numbers: build number routes using strict movement rules.
  • Summit Numbers: balance target values on a wrap-around board.
  • Entropy: Zero: place geometric repairs to stop spreading corruption.
  • Bitcoin Puzzle Quest: test offline keys in a public puzzle simulator.
  • Astrovert: time your moves to survive a cosmic run.

Information is more satisfying than instruction

The best puzzles do not constantly tell the player what to do. They give the player information and let them make the discovery.

Minesweeper is built almost entirely from this idea. A revealed number does not tell you where to click next. It tells you how many mines touch that square. One clue is simple. Several overlapping clues become a logic problem.

A “1” beside one covered square is obvious. A cluster of 1s, 2s and 3s around an irregular frontier can force deduction, probability and risk management. You are not learning a new rule. You are learning how much meaning the existing rule contains.

Minesweeper has also been studied formally. Richard Kaye showed that Minesweeper is NP-complete, by connecting Minesweeper consistency problems to classic computational complexity questions. (Springer)

Again, the design point is not that players need to know complexity theory. They do not. The point is that a tiny local rule, “this number counts adjacent mines,” can scale into a hard global problem.

That is one of the signatures of a strong puzzle: the player always understands the rule, but not always the implication.

The player should feel ownership of the solution

A satisfying puzzle gives the player the feeling that the answer was earned, not handed over.

This is why simple rules are so powerful. When the rule set is clear, the player knows the solution came from their own attention. A Sudoku digit falls into place because they noticed a constraint. A Wordle answer appears because they interpreted the feedback well. A Minesweeper square becomes safe because they understood the numbers. A Tetris recovery works because they planned several pieces ahead.

The pleasure is not just “winning.” It is the moment when confusion turns into structure.

That is the “aha” moment and it depends on clarity. If the rules are messy, the player may think, “I finally understood what the game wanted.” If the rules are clean, the player thinks, “I finally saw it.”

That second feeling is much stronger.

Simple does not mean shallow

2048 looks almost absurdly simple: swipe tiles, merge matching numbers, try to reach 2048. But a 4-by-4 board fills quickly and every swipe affects the whole board. A move is not just a move. It is a commitment to a future shape.

Researchers have shown that generalized versions of 2048 are computationally difficult too. Rahul Mehta proved that a variant of 2048 on an n-by-n board is PSPACE-complete under specific conditions. (arXiv)

For designers, this is useful because 2048 demonstrates a very economical kind of depth. There are no enemies, levels, dialogue trees or special abilities. There is just a board, a movement rule and a growth rule. Yet from those ingredients you get planning, greed, risk, recovery and failure.

A shallow puzzle runs out once the player understands the trick. A deep puzzle begins when the player understands the trick.

Good puzzle design removes noise

A hard puzzle does not need to look hard. In fact, it often works better when it looks approachable.

This is where many puzzle designs go wrong. They confuse complexity with clutter. They add symbols, exceptions, modes, bonuses, edge cases and special rules, hoping that more material will create more depth. Sometimes it does. Often it just makes the player tired.

Classic puzzles tend to be ruthless about removing noise. Crosswords are clues and a grid. Sudoku is digits and constraints. Wordle is guesses and feedback. Tetris is pieces and gravity. Minesweeper is numbers and hidden mines.

That cleanliness matters because the player’s mental effort should go into the problem, not the instructions. The puzzle should not say, “Look how many rules I have.” It should say, “You already know enough. Now look closer.”

Why simple-rule puzzles last

Simple-rule puzzles last because they are easy to re-enter.

You can leave Sudoku for a month and still remember how it works. You can explain Wordle to someone in less than a minute. Tetris does not require lore. Minesweeper does not need a tutorial campaign. Chess has centuries of theory, but the basic object is still recognizable: protect your king, threaten theirs.

This makes these puzzles unusually durable. They fit into daily life. They work during a commute, over coffee, before bed or between meetings. They reward five minutes of attention, but they can also support years of mastery.

That combination is rare. Many games are easy to start but easy to exhaust. Others are deep but hard to enter. The best simple-rule puzzles hit the valuable middle:

You can begin immediately, but you cannot master them immediately.

The real formula

Simple rules create hard puzzles when three things are true.

First, the rule must be clear. The player should understand what is allowed, what is forbidden and what success looks like.

Second, the rule must create consequences. A move should change the future state of the puzzle in a meaningful way.

Third, the system must produce interaction. Clues, moves or constraints should overlap so that the whole puzzle becomes more than a collection of separate parts.

That is why Wordle, Sudoku, Tetris, Minesweeper, Chess and 2048 are not just “simple games.” They are compact systems. Their rules are small enough to learn quickly, but strong enough to generate surprise.

A great puzzle does not need a thick rulebook. It needs a clear idea, strong constraints and room for consequences to unfold.

The puzzle says: you know enough to begin.

Then it waits to see how far you can think.
