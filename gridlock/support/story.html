The Story of GridLock Numbers

The most elegant puzzles are built on a single, unforgiving constraint. GridLock Numbers has one: visit every cell exactly once, without ever getting trapped. It sounds like a rule a child could understand. The mathematics behind it occupied some of history's greatest minds for centuries.

The Knight's Tour

On a standard chessboard, a knight moves in an L-shape, two squares along one axis, one square along the other. A Knight's Tour is a sequence of knight moves that visits every square on the board exactly once. The question of whether such a tour exists, and how to find one, is one of the oldest problems in recreational mathematics.

References to the knight's tour appear in Sanskrit manuscripts as early as the 9th century CE. The Arab polymath al-Adlī ar-Rūmī described solutions around 840 CE. But it was Leonhard Euler who first treated it as a serious mathematical object in 1759, publishing a systematic method for constructing tours, including closed tours, where the knight ends on a square one move away from where it began, forming a perfect loop.

"The knight's tour is not merely a curiosity of the chessboard. It is a window into the structure of all path-finding problems."
— Paraphrase of Euler's working notes, 1759

Hamiltonian Paths and the Cost of Completeness

In 1857, the Irish mathematician William Rowan Hamilton generalized the idea. He defined what we now call a Hamiltonian path: a path through a graph that visits every vertex exactly once. The knight's tour is one specific instance, the graph is the chessboard, the vertices are squares, the edges are legal knight moves.

Hamilton even sold a puzzle based on the concept, the Icosian Game, where players traced paths along the edges of a dodecahedron. It was one of the first commercially sold mathematical puzzles. It was also, by modern standards, NP-complete: no efficient general algorithm is known for deciding whether a Hamiltonian path exists in an arbitrary graph, let alone for finding one.

The Geometry of Getting Stuck

What makes these path problems so viscerally difficult is the way traps accumulate silently. Early moves feel free, the board is open, options are plentiful. But each placement quietly forecloses future routes. By the time a dead end becomes visible, the choices that caused it were made five or ten moves ago. The board hasn't changed. Your opportunity to fix it has vanished.

This is the phenomenon that mathematicians call local versus global reasoning. A locally optimal move, one that looks fine right now, can be globally catastrophic. The notorious Warnsdorff's rule, discovered in 1823, offers a heuristic for avoiding this: always move to the square with the fewest onward options. By prioritizing the most constrained positions early, you keep the rest of the board accessible. It works remarkably well and it rewards exactly the kind of forward-looking play that GridLock demands.

Translating the Tour into GridLock

GridLock Numbers is a direct descendant of the knight's tour in mechanic and spirit. The movement rules, two cells for orthogonal moves, one cell for diagonals, encode the same L-shaped leap in a more generalized form. The goal is the same: number every cell from 1 to the board's total, visiting each exactly once, never retracing a step.

What GridLock adds is a scoring dimension the classical puzzle lacks. Bonus x2 cells reward players who route high-value numbers through specific positions, turning the path problem into a simultaneous optimization challenge. You are not just looking for any valid tour. You are looking for a tour that passes through the right cells at the right moment. The difficulty levels (Easy, Medium, Hard) tighten the board and raise the stakes, until Hard mode becomes a genuine exercise in sustained spatial foresight.

The undo mechanic is also deliberate. Classical knight's tour solvers backtrack computationally, they reverse bad decisions at negligible cost. GridLock gives you the same escape valve, but makes it costly. Every undo is a small concession: the path was wrong, the foresight was incomplete. Used sparingly, it rescues a run. Used freely, it erodes the score.

Every number you place is a commitment. Every cell you skip past is a door you may never reopen.

GridLock Numbers asks whether you can see the whole board from the very first move and find the path that Euler, Hamilton and Warnsdorff spent lifetimes trying to describe.
