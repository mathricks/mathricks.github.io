The Story of Summit Numbers

DESIGNER NOTES // PROBLEM ORIGINS // FILE: S-01

Every puzzle game has a mathematical soul. For Summit Numbers, that soul was forged in one of computer science's oldest and most deceptively simple questions: can you find a combination of numbers that add up to exactly the right total?

The Knapsack Problem

Imagine you're a mountaineer preparing for an ascent. Your pack can carry a fixed weight. Spread before you are supplies of varying bulk and value. Which ones do you bring? You want to maximize what you carry without exceeding what you can bear. This is the Knapsack Problem, a deceptively clean question that has occupied mathematicians and computer scientists for well over a century.

The formal version was studied as early as 1897 by W. J. Mathews, and later popularized by George Dantzig, the pioneer of linear programming. Strip away the optimization layer, i.e. forget about maximizing value, and you arrive at the raw heart of the problem: the Subset Sum question. Given a set of integers, can you find a subset that sums to a specific target?

"A problem worthy of attack proves its worth by fighting back."
— Piet Hein

Why It's So Hard

The Subset Sum problem belongs to a notorious class of computational problems called NP-complete. This means there is no known algorithm that can efficiently solve all instances of it as the numbers grow. With just 10 items, there are over a thousand possible subsets. With 50 items, more than a quadrillion. The explosion is exponential.

Richard Karp included it in his landmark 1972 paper identifying 21 NP-complete problems, cementing its place in the pantheon of hard problems. The difficulty is not that it's impossible to solve, for small, well-chosen numbers it can feel almost trivial. The difficulty is that the hardness is unpredictable. The same structure that makes one instance easy makes another brutally resistant.

The Partition Beneath the Summit

A closely related puzzle is the Partition Problem: split a set of numbers into two groups so that each group sums to the same total. It sounds almost frivolous, dividing things equally, yet it is equally NP-complete and surfaces everywhere, from load balancing in distributed computing to fairly dividing assets in legal disputes.

What makes these problems so fertile for game design is their texture. Unlike a maze, which has a clear topology, a subset sum puzzle offers no natural gradient to guide your intuition. You can feel equally lost at move one as at move ten. And then, suddenly, you see it. The right combination of pieces snapping into place with the satisfying click of inevitability. The solution was always there, hiding in plain arithmetic.

Forging a Game from the Problem

Summit Numbers was designed to bottle that specific feeling. The board wraps around. Your pieces don't fall off edges, they reappear on the other side. Movement is chess-like and deliberate. Each piece you place carries a value, and the four borders of the board keep a running tally of everything passing through them: Top, Right, Bottom, Left.

Your goal is to hit all four targets simultaneously. Not one at a time, all at once. This constraint turns a simple addition exercise into a genuine spatial reasoning challenge. You must think about which pieces contribute to which sums, plan movement paths that serve multiple targets at once, and merge identical values to consolidate your options. Every round is, at its core, a four-dimensional subset sum problem encoded in motion.

The targets are not completely random. Each round is carefully generated so that a solution always exists, the numbers are chosen with the partition structure in mind, ensuring that what feels impossible is merely difficult. This is the design contract with the player: the summit is always reachable. Whether you find the path is up to you.

The moment all four targets click to zero is the moment the math becomes joy.

That's the promise of Summit Numbers. Not a brute-force exercise in arithmetic, but a compact, elegant duel with one of mathematics' most ancient hard problems, solved in rounds short enough to fit in your pocket.
